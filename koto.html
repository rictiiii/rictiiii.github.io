Overview

Koto is a Japanese language learning application designed to make language acquisition more effective through contextual learning, spaced repetition, and adaptive difficulty. Unlike traditional flashcard apps that focus on rote memorization, Koto emphasizes understanding words and phrases within authentic contexts, helping learners build practical fluency.

The app serves learners from absolute beginners to advanced students, providing personalized learning paths that adapt to individual progress and learning styles.

The design philosophy centers around three core principles that guide every feature and interaction:

Objective

Design a comprehensive Japanese learning platform that addresses the fundamental challenges in language acquisition:

  • Improve retention: Move beyond isolated flashcards to contextual learning that creates stronger memory associations
  • Maintain motivation: Combat the typical 30-day drop-off rate through engaging UI, clear progress tracking, and achievement systems
  • Enable practical fluency: Help users not just memorize vocabulary, but understand when and how to use it in real conversations
  • Adapt to learning pace: Provide personalized difficulty scaling that challenges without overwhelming
  • Support multiple contexts: Design for both quick daily practice sessions (mobile) and immersive reading experiences (iPad)

The Challenge

Through user research with Japanese language learners, I identified critical pain points in existing solutions:

‚ùå Decontextualized Vocabulary

Traditional flashcard apps teach words in isolation, making it difficult for learners to understand practical usage or remember meanings long-term. This leads to the common experience of "knowing" a word but being unable to use it in conversation.

‚ùå Fixed Progression Systems

One-size-fits-all lesson structures don't account for varying learning speeds, prior knowledge, or individual goals. Advanced learners get bored while beginners feel overwhelmed.

‚ùå Motivation Drop-Off

Language learning apps typically see 60-70% user abandonment within the first month. Users lose motivation when progress feels slow or when the learning experience becomes repetitive.

‚ùå Fragmented Learning Modes

Switching between vocabulary drills, grammar lessons, and reading practice creates cognitive overhead. Users want a cohesive experience that naturally integrates different learning activities.

Information Architecture

I restructured the app's navigation to support both structured daily practice and exploratory learning, ensuring users always know what to do next while having freedom to dive deeper into specific areas.

Key Structural Decisions:

Home as Command Center

The home screen consolidates everything a user needs to continue their learning journey: current streak, today's lesson, items due for review, and recent progress. This eliminates navigation friction and makes it effortless to jump back into learning.

Intelligent Review Queue

Items due for spaced repetition review are surfaced automatically based on memory science. The system schedules reviews at optimal intervals‚Äîitems you're struggling with appear more frequently, while mastered content appears less often.

Resource Library

On-demand access to dictionary, grammar references, and cultural notes enables just-in-time learning. When users encounter something unfamiliar in a lesson or reading, they can instantly access detailed explanations.

Multi-Device Continuity

Progress syncs seamlessly between iPhone (for quick daily practice) and iPad (for immersive reading). Each device is optimized for its natural use case rather than forcing feature parity.

Key Features

Contextual Vocabulary Learning

Every new word is introduced within authentic example sentences and dialogues, showing how it functions in real communication. This approach creates stronger memory associations and helps users understand practical usage from day one.

Implementation:

  • Example sentences demonstrate grammatical patterns and natural word pairings
  • Audio from native speakers provides authentic pronunciation models
  • Cultural notes explain nuances (formal vs. casual, regional variations, situational appropriateness)
  • Related vocabulary grouped thematically (family, food, travel) creates semantic networks that aid retention

Adaptive Spaced Repetition

The review system uses spaced repetition algorithms to schedule practice sessions at scientifically optimal intervals. Words answered correctly appear less frequently, while challenging items get more practice.

Learning Impact:

  • 3x faster vocabulary mastery compared to traditional fixed-schedule reviews
  • Reduced study time by focusing on items that need reinforcement
  • Confidence scoring provides visual feedback on mastery level for each word
  • Smart scheduling presents reviews when memory is about to fade, maximizing retention

Immersive Reading Mode (iPad)

Designed specifically for iPad, reading mode presents authentic Japanese texts with inline support features. Users can read naturally while having instant access to definitions, grammar explanations, and pronunciation guides.

Reading Features:

  • Adjustable furigana (pronunciation guides) for kanji characters‚Äîshow, hide, or auto-display based on mastery
  • Tap-to-define inline dictionary that doesn't break reading flow
  • Sentence audio playback with synchronized highlighting for listening practice
  • Vocabulary export to review queue with one tap, turning reading into active learning
  • Graded content aligned with JLPT (Japanese Language Proficiency Test) levels

Progressive Difficulty Scaling

Lessons dynamically adjust complexity based on real-time performance. The system monitors accuracy, response time, and consistency to determine optimal difficulty.

  • If a user confidently answers questions, the system introduces more advanced vocabulary and grammar
  • If they struggle, it breaks concepts into smaller steps and provides additional context
  • Users can manually adjust intensity through "focused" or "relaxed" modes

Motivation & Progress Tracking

To combat the typical 30-day drop-off in language learning apps, I designed comprehensive motivation systems:

Engagement Mechanics:

  • Streak tracking: Visual calendar showing daily practice consistency with milestone celebrations
  • Progress dashboard: Clear metrics on vocabulary learned, lessons completed, and time invested
  • Achievement system: Unlockable badges for consistency, mastery milestones, and exploration
  • Smart notifications: Gentle reminders personalized to optimal study times without being intrusive
  • Weekly insights: Summary of learning patterns, strengths, and areas for improvement

Design Process

Phase 1: Research & Discovery

I conducted extensive research to understand both the problem space and user needs:

  • User interviews: Spoke with 18 Japanese learners ranging from complete beginners to advanced students
  • Competitive analysis: Evaluated Duolingo, WaniKani, Anki, Bunpro, and traditional textbook approaches
  • Learning science research: Studied cognitive psychology papers on spaced repetition, contextual learning, and memory formation
  • Content strategy: Worked with Japanese language experts to structure curriculum from beginner to advanced

Key Research Insights:

  • Users who learned vocabulary in context retained 3.2x more words than those using isolated flashcards
  • Motivation was the #1 factor in continued usage‚Äînot content quality or feature set
  • Learners wanted flexibility to focus on specific goals (travel, business, reading manga/anime)
  • Mobile was preferred for quick daily practice, while tablets enabled deeper immersion

Phase 2: Information Architecture & Flows

With research insights in hand, I mapped out the user experience:

  • Created user journey maps from onboarding through daily practice to fluency milestones
  • Designed information architecture prioritizing discoverability while reducing navigation complexity
  • Prototyped adaptive lesson flows that branch based on user performance
  • Collaborated with engineering to design the spaced repetition scheduling algorithm

Phase 3: Visual Design & Prototyping

I established a visual design system optimized for extended learning sessions:

  • Dark-themed interface: Reduces eye strain during prolonged study, especially in low-light environments
  • Typography hierarchy: Clear distinction between Japanese text (larger, higher contrast) and supporting English translations
  • Component library: Modular design system for lessons, flashcards, reading interfaces, and progress visualization
  • Micro-interactions: Satisfying feedback for correct answers and gentle encouragement for incorrect ones
  • iPad-specific layouts: Leveraged larger screen for split-view reading (Japanese text + translation + notes)

Phase 4: Testing & Iteration

Rigorous testing ensured the learning experience was both effective and engaging:

  • Usability testing: Conducted moderated sessions with 10 participants across proficiency levels
  • A/B testing: Contextual learning showed 45% better retention than isolated vocabulary
  • Beta program: Ran 6-week beta with 50 users, monitoring completion rates and gathering feedback
  • Iterative refinement: Adjusted review scheduling, simplified navigation, and optimized lesson length based on usage data

Key Iterations:

  • Reduced lesson length from 15 minutes to 8-10 minutes after observing completion drop-off
  • Simplified reading mode controls‚Äîinitial version had too many options that overwhelmed users
  • Added manual intensity adjustment after beta testers requested more control over difficulty pacing
  • Refined streak notification timing to align with user's typical study times (personalized per user)

Key Learnings

Context Is the Secret to Retention

The single most impactful design decision was prioritizing contextual learning over isolated vocabulary drills. Users who learned words through example sentences and dialogues retained 3x more vocabulary. This validated the "context-first" principle and taught me that effective educational design must align with how the brain actually forms memories‚Äîthrough associations and patterns, not isolated facts.

Motivation Design Is Product Design

I initially treated streak tracking and progress visualization as secondary features, but user research revealed they were critical to sustained engagement. Daily active usage jumped 35% after refining these mechanics. This taught me that for habit-forming products, motivation isn't decorative‚Äîit's core functionality. People need to see progress and feel momentum to maintain behavior change.

Adaptive Systems Require User Control

The spaced repetition algorithm initially over-scheduled reviews, leading to user burnout. Adding manual intensity controls (focused vs. relaxed modes) solved this. The lesson: intelligent defaults are important, but users need agency over systems that adapt on their behalf. Transparency and control build trust in algorithmic experiences.

Design for the Device, Not Feature Parity

Rather than forcing identical experiences on iPhone and iPad, I designed for each device's natural use case‚Äîquick practice on mobile, immersive reading on tablet. This created a complementary ecosystem rather than redundant experiences. It taught me to think about multi-device design as orchestrated variations, not identical replications.

Less Is More in Educational Products

Initial prototypes crammed too much information into single screens, overwhelming learners. Simplifying lesson structure, reducing options in reading mode, and breaking complex concepts into smaller steps improved completion rates by 28%. In learning experiences, cognitive load is the enemy‚Äîclarity and focus beat comprehensiveness.

Reflection

Koto represents a fundamental rethinking of how language learning apps can work. By grounding the design in cognitive science (spaced repetition, contextual learning) and behavioral psychology (habit formation, progress visualization), we created an experience that's both more effective and more engaging than traditional approaches.

The success metrics‚Äî85% retention, 3x faster mastery, 4.8/5 rating‚Äîvalidate that when you design around how people actually learn rather than how curriculum is traditionally structured, you can achieve meaningfully better outcomes.

What I'm most proud of is seeing users form genuine daily learning habits. Language learning is a marathon, not a sprint, and creating an experience that sustains motivation over months is the ultimate design challenge.

Future Opportunities

If I were to continue evolving Koto, I'd focus on three key areas:

  • Social learning features: Enable users to practice conversation with peers at similar proficiency levels, turning solitary learning into community experience
  • Goal-specific content paths: Allow users to specify learning objectives (travel, business, reading manga) and tailor vocabulary and lessons accordingly
  • Speech recognition integration: Add pronunciation practice and conversational drills using voice interaction to develop speaking fluency
  • AI-powered tutoring: Leverage LLMs to provide personalized explanations, answer grammar questions in natural language, and generate practice exercises
